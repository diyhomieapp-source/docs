The kitchen is the heart of your home — and one of the best places to invest your DIY energy. Even modest upgrades like fresh cabinet paint or a new backsplash can dramatically improve how your kitchen looks and feels, all while adding measurable resale value. You don't need to tear out cabinets or hire a contractor to make a real impact. With the right tools, materials, and guidance, most homeowners can complete meaningful kitchen upgrades over a weekend or two.

## Popular DIY Kitchen Upgrades

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Painting Kitchen Cabinets — Intermediate">
    Painting your cabinets is the single highest-impact kitchen upgrade you can make without replacing them. A fresh coat of paint in a modern color — think soft white, navy, sage green, or warm gray — can make a dated kitchen look brand new. Expect to spend one to two weekends on this project. Proper prep is everything: sanding, cleaning, and priming are what separate a professional-looking finish from a peeling mess.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Installing a Tile Backsplash — Intermediate">
    A tile backsplash adds color, texture, and personality to your kitchen while protecting the wall behind your counters. Subway tile is the most beginner-friendly option due to its simple layout, but you can also work with mosaic sheets, herringbone patterns, or large-format tiles. The job requires tile adhesive or mastic, grout, a tile cutter or wet saw, spacers, and patience. Budget a full weekend for a standard 15–20 square foot area.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Replacing Cabinet Hardware — Beginner">
    Swapping out old knobs and pulls is the easiest kitchen upgrade you can make — and one of the most effective. New hardware in brushed brass, matte black, or brushed nickel can completely change the feel of your kitchen in an afternoon. All you need is a screwdriver. The only challenge is matching the existing hole spacing (standard is 3" or 96mm for pulls) or filling and re-drilling holes if you're switching styles.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Installing Under-Cabinet Lighting — Beginner to Intermediate">
    Under-cabinet lighting adds task lighting over your counters and creates a warm, layered ambiance. Plug-in LED strip lights are the easiest option — no wiring required, just peel and stick, then plug in. Hardwired puck lights or integrated LED bars offer a cleaner look but require basic electrical work. Most plug-in systems can be completed in under two hours and make a surprisingly large visual impact, especially in darker kitchens.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Replacing a Kitchen Faucet — Beginner to Intermediate">
    A new kitchen faucet is a functional and aesthetic upgrade that most homeowners can tackle in two to three hours. Modern pull-down faucets with sprayer heads are practical and widely available. You'll need an adjustable wrench, basin wrench (for tight spaces under the sink), plumber's tape, and the new faucet. Turn off the water supply valves under the sink before you start. Most faucets come with clear installation instructions.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Resurfacing Countertops — Beginner">
    If full countertop replacement isn't in the budget, resurfacing is a creative and cost-effective alternative. Contact paper in marble, butcher block, or concrete patterns can be applied directly over laminate surfaces and looks surprisingly convincing. Alternatively, countertop paint kits (like Rust-Oleum's countertop coating) can give laminate or tile counters a stone-like appearance. These solutions aren't permanent, but they're excellent for rentals or bridge updates before a larger renovation.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Installing a New Sink — Intermediate">
    Replacing a drop-in kitchen sink is a manageable DIY project that pairs well with a faucet upgrade. You'll need to disconnect the drain, garbage disposal (if present), and water supply lines before lifting out the old sink. A stainless steel drop-in sink is the easiest to install since it simply drops into the existing cutout. Undermount sinks are more complex and typically require professional countertop modification. Always apply silicone sealant around the perimeter of a drop-in sink before setting it.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Painting Kitchen Cabinets: A Deep Dive

Cabinet painting is the most transformative DIY kitchen upgrade available, but it rewards patience and proper preparation. Here's how to do it right.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Remove Cabinet Doors and Hardware">
    Use a screwdriver to remove all cabinet doors, drawers, and drawer fronts. Label each door with a piece of painter's tape on the back (e.g., "Upper Left 1") so you know exactly where each one goes back. Remove all hinges, knobs, and pulls and store them in labeled bags — or set them aside if you're replacing the hardware.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Clean All Surfaces Thoroughly">
    Kitchen cabinet surfaces accumulate grease, cooking residue, and grime that will prevent paint from bonding. Wash all surfaces — doors, drawer fronts, and cabinet boxes — with a degreaser like TSP substitute or a dish soap and water solution. Rinse clean and allow everything to dry completely before moving on.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Sand All Surfaces">
    Lightly sand all paintable surfaces with 120-grit sandpaper to scuff up the existing finish and give the primer something to grip. You don't need to strip the cabinets down to bare wood — just break the gloss. Follow up with 150-grit for a smoother surface. Wipe away all sanding dust with a tack cloth or damp microfiber cloth.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Apply Primer">
    Prime all surfaces with a shellac-based or bonding primer. This step is non-negotiable — skipping primer is the most common reason DIY cabinet paint jobs fail. Apply primer to cabinet boxes first (using a brush for edges and a small foam roller for flat surfaces), then prime all doors and drawer fronts laid flat on a work surface. Allow the primer to dry fully per the manufacturer's instructions, then lightly sand with 220-grit and wipe clean.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Paint the Cabinet Doors and Drawer Fronts">
    Apply your finish coat to the doors and drawer fronts while they are still flat. Two thin coats are better than one thick coat — thin coats dry faster, level better, and are less prone to drips. Allow the first coat to dry completely before applying the second. Lightly sand between coats with 220-grit for the smoothest finish.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Paint the Cabinet Boxes">
    With doors still off, paint the cabinet boxes in place. Start with the interiors (if painting them), then move to the face frames. Use a quality angled brush for corners and edges and a foam roller for the flat faces. Two coats, sanding lightly between coats.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Reassemble the Cabinets">
    Once all paint is fully cured (check manufacturer's cure time — this is different from dry time), rehang the doors and reinstall drawer fronts. Take your time aligning hinges so doors hang straight. Adjust hinge screws as needed to get even gaps between doors.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Install New Hardware">
    Finish with new knobs or pulls for the full effect. Use a hardware template (available at home improvement stores) to mark hole locations consistently across all doors and drawers. Drill carefully with a sharp bit and a scrap wood backer to prevent blowout.
  </Step>
</Steps>

### Best Paint Types for Cabinets

Not all paints hold up equally well on cabinets, which take a beating from daily use, humidity, and cleaning.

- **Alkyd (oil-based) paint** — The traditional choice for cabinets. Extremely hard, durable finish with excellent leveling (brush marks flow out beautifully). Longer dry times and requires mineral spirits for cleanup, but the result is outstanding.
- **Cabinet enamel (water-based alkyd hybrid)** — The modern favorite. Products like Benjamin Moore Advance or Sherwin-Williams Emerald Urethane Trim Enamel offer near-alkyd hardness with water-based convenience. Excellent leveling, durable finish, and easy cleanup with soap and water.
- **Avoid standard latex wall paint** — It's too soft for cabinet surfaces and will dent, scratch, and peel with regular use.

<Tip>
  For the smoothest possible finish on cabinet doors, consider renting or purchasing an HVLP (high-volume, low-pressure) paint sprayer. Spray-painted cabinet doors have a factory-smooth appearance that's nearly impossible to achieve with a brush and roller. Set up a temporary spray booth in your garage using drop cloths and cardboard, hang doors from hooks using wire through the hinge holes, and spray thin, even coats. Practice on cardboard first to dial in your spray pattern and distance.
</Tip>

## Backsplash Installation Overview

A backsplash protects your walls and serves as a focal point in the kitchen. You have several material options depending on your budget, skill level, and desired look.

### Backsplash Options

| Option | Difficulty | Cost | Durability |
|---|---|---|---|
| Classic ceramic tile | Intermediate | $$ | Excellent |
| Glass or mosaic tile | Intermediate | $$$ | Excellent |
| Peel-and-stick tile | Beginner | $ | Good (renters/budget) |
| Shiplap or wood planks | Intermediate | $$ | Good (seal well) |

### Tools and Materials Checklist

- [ ] Tile or backsplash material of choice
- [ ] Tile adhesive, mastic, or peel-and-stick backing (per material)
- [ ] Notched trowel (for mastic application)
- [ ] Tile spacers (typically 1/16" for subway tile)
- [ ] Tile cutter or wet saw (for straight cuts); nippers (for curves)
- [ ] Grout and grout float
- [ ] Grout sealer
- [ ] Painter's tape (to protect counters and outlets)
- [ ] Level and chalk line
- [ ] Measuring tape and pencil
- [ ] Sponge and buckets for cleanup

<Warning>
  **Permits required for certain kitchen upgrades.** Adding new electrical outlets, moving or adding plumbing lines, or installing a range hood with new ductwork typically requires a building permit and must be done (or inspected) by a licensed professional in most jurisdictions. Always check with your local building department before starting any work that involves electrical wiring, gas lines, or plumbing rough-in. Unpermitted work can complicate home sales and void homeowner's insurance.
</Warning>

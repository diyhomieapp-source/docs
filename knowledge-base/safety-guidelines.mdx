Every project in the DIYHomie library starts with the same assumption: your safety comes before the project. No tile backsplash, painted cabinet, or new deck board is worth an injury — and the overwhelming majority of DIY accidents are preventable with the right knowledge, equipment, and habits. Whether you're a first-time DIYer picking up a drill for the first time or an experienced homeowner tackling a complex renovation, these safety guidelines apply to every job, every time. Read this page before you start any new type of project, and revisit it whenever you're working with unfamiliar tools, chemicals, or materials.

## Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

PPE is your last line of defense when something unexpected happens — a chip of tile flies, a chemical splashes, a power tool kicks back. Wearing the right gear is not optional; it's the baseline.

### Safety Glasses and Goggles

Wear safety glasses or goggles any time you are cutting, grinding, drilling, nailing, chiseling, or working with materials that can produce flying debris. Standard prescription glasses are **not** a substitute — they don't provide side protection and can shatter in ways that safety-rated lenses won't. ANSI Z87.1-rated safety glasses cost under $10 and should live in your toolbox permanently.

- **Safety glasses** — suitable for most workshop and general DIY tasks
- **Safety goggles** — use when working with liquids, chemicals, or in situations with debris flying from multiple directions (e.g., using an angle grinder)

### Work Gloves

Match your gloves to the hazard:

- **Cut-resistant gloves** — for working with sheet metal, sharp tile edges, glass, or handling freshly cut lumber
- **Chemical-resistant rubber gloves** — for working with solvents, strippers, adhesives, concrete, and cleaning chemicals
- **Leather or general work gloves** — for rough materials, handling lumber, landscaping, and general work
- **Latex or nitrile gloves** — for painting and light chemical work where dexterity matters

Note that gloves are **not** always appropriate around rotating power tools (lathes, drill presses). The glove can catch the spinning part and pull your hand in — assess each situation.

### Dust Masks and Respirators

Breathing protection is critical and frequently overlooked. Many of the materials DIYers work with produce particles and vapors that cause serious long-term health effects.

- **N95 disposable respirator** — the minimum for drywall dust, insulation fibers, sanding dust (wood and painted surfaces), and light painting tasks
- **Half-face respirator with P100 particulate filters** — for heavy sanding, cutting cement board, tile cutting (silica dust), and demolition work
- **Half-face respirator with organic vapor cartridges** — for solvent-based paints, lacquers, varnishes, adhesives, and chemical strippers
- **Combination P100 + organic vapor cartridges** — the best all-around option for most renovation work

A dust mask with a metal nose clip that seals to your face is far better than a surgical-style paper mask, which provides minimal protection against fine particles.

### Hearing Protection

Sustained noise above 85 decibels causes permanent, cumulative hearing damage — and most power tools exceed this threshold. Use foam earplugs (NRR 25–33) or earmuffs (NRR 20–30) when operating:

- Circular saws, miter saws, table saws
- Angle grinders
- Routers and planers
- Nail guns
- Hammer drills and demolition hammers
- Shop vacuums (extended use)

If you have to raise your voice to be heard by someone two feet away, the noise level is high enough to require protection.

### Non-Slip Footwear

Wear closed-toe, non-slip footwear on every worksite — no sandals, no bare feet, no flip-flops. Work boots with steel toes offer the best protection against dropped tools and materials. At minimum, wear sturdy athletic or work shoes with rubber soles that grip well. On slippery surfaces (wet decks, freshly mopped floors), be especially cautious and take shorter steps.

## Ladder Safety

Falls from ladders are one of the leading causes of serious home injury. Most ladder accidents are preventable and result from rushing, overreaching, or using the wrong equipment.

### The 3-Point Contact Rule

Always maintain three points of contact with the ladder — two hands and one foot, or two feet and one hand. This means you should never carry tools or materials up a ladder in your hands. Use a tool belt, apron, or a bucket hook to bring what you need up with you.

### Never Overreach

Your belt buckle should never go outside the rails of the ladder. If you need to reach something that requires leaning out, climb down and reposition the ladder. Overreaching is the single most common cause of ladder tip-overs.

### Set the Ladder at the Proper Angle

For extension ladders, use the **1:4 ratio** — for every 4 feet of height, the base of the ladder should be 1 foot out from the wall. A ladder set too steep can tip backward; a ladder set too shallow can slide out at the base. The quick test: stand at the base with your toes touching the feet of the ladder and extend your arms straight out — you should be able to rest your hands on the rung at shoulder height.

### Use the Right Ladder for the Job

- All ladders are rated for a maximum load — **your weight plus all of your tools and materials**. Check the duty rating label on the ladder.
  - Type III (Light Duty): 200 lbs
  - Type II (Medium Duty): 225 lbs
  - Type I (Heavy Duty): 250 lbs
  - Type IA (Extra Heavy Duty): 300 lbs
  - Type IAA (Special Duty): 375 lbs
- Never stand on the top two rungs of a stepladder or the top three rungs of an extension ladder.
- On uneven ground, use a ladder leveler — don't prop the feet on boards or bricks.

## Power Tool Safety

Power tools enable incredible work — and demand consistent respect. These rules apply to every power tool, every time.

- **Inspect before use.** Check power cords for cuts, fraying, or damaged insulation. Inspect guards and safety features before plugging in. Never use a tool with a bypassed guard.
- **Disconnect before changing blades or bits.** Unplug the tool or remove the battery before changing a saw blade, drill bit, router bit, or any cutting accessory. This is non-negotiable — "the trigger wasn't pressed" is not a reliable safety measure.
- **Keep fingers away from cutting paths.** Always know where the blade or bit will exit the material. Clamp your workpiece rather than holding it with your free hand when possible.
- **Secure your workpiece.** Use clamps, a vise, or a non-slip mat to hold the material you're cutting or drilling. A workpiece that moves unexpectedly causes most tool accidents.
- **Dress for the tool.** Tie back long hair, remove loose jewelry, and avoid loose sleeves when operating any rotating or cutting tool.
- **Let the tool do the work.** Forcing a power tool (pushing a saw too hard, over-torquing a drill) causes binding, kickback, and blade/bit damage. Use sharp blades and bits — dull tools require more force and behave more unpredictably.

## Chemical Safety

Many common DIY products — paints, stains, adhesives, strippers, solvents, and cleaners — contain chemicals that require careful handling.

### Ventilation

Always work in a well-ventilated area when using solvent-based products. Open windows and doors, run fans to create cross-ventilation, and take regular fresh-air breaks. Many solvent vapors are heavier than air and accumulate at floor level — don't assume that because you can't smell them strongly, they're not present.

### Read the Safety Data Sheet (SDS)

Every chemical product has a Safety Data Sheet available from the manufacturer (and legally required to be available at point of sale). The SDS tells you what hazards the product presents, what PPE is required, how to handle spills, what first aid measures to take in case of exposure, and how to dispose of the product properly. Before using any chemical for the first time, read its SDS.

### Proper Disposal

Do not pour paint, solvents, adhesives, or chemical strippers down household drains or into storm drains. Many of these products are regulated as household hazardous waste (HHW). Most municipalities offer HHW collection events or permanent drop-off facilities. Check your local public works or waste management website for disposal options.

- **Latex paint** — can often be dried out (leave the lid off in a well-ventilated area) and disposed of in regular trash once fully solid, in many jurisdictions
- **Oil-based paint, stain, varnish, and lacquer** — HHW disposal required
- **Paint thinner and solvents** — HHW disposal required
- **Aerosol cans** — many municipalities accept fully empty cans in recycling; partially full cans are HHW

## Hazardous Materials in Older Homes

If your home was built before 1980, there's a meaningful chance it contains one or both of the following hazardous materials. Knowing where they may be present — and what NOT to do — is critical before starting any renovation or demolition work.

### Asbestos

Asbestos was widely used in building materials through the late 1970s due to its fire resistance and insulating properties. Common locations include:

- Popcorn ceilings (textured spray-on ceilings)
- 9"×9" vinyl floor tiles and the adhesive beneath them
- Pipe and duct insulation (often gray or white wrap)
- Exterior transite siding and roofing shingles
- Joint compound and drywall tape (pre-1980)
- Insulation around boilers and furnaces

<Warning>
  **Do not disturb materials that may contain asbestos without testing first.** Asbestos is harmless when intact and undisturbed — it only becomes a health hazard when fibers are released into the air through cutting, sanding, scraping, or demolition. If you suspect a material contains asbestos, hire a certified asbestos inspector to test it before proceeding. Disturbing asbestos without proper containment and removal protocols is illegal in many jurisdictions and carries serious health risks.
</Warning>

### Lead Paint

Lead-based paint was used in the majority of homes built before 1978. It's most commonly found on older trim, doors, windows, and exterior surfaces. Intact, well-adhered lead paint is generally safe — the hazard arises from sanding, scraping, or otherwise disturbing it.

<Warning>
  **Follow EPA RRP (Renovation, Repair, and Painting) guidelines when disturbing painted surfaces in pre-1978 homes.** This includes using protective sheeting, wearing proper respirators, wet-sanding methods to minimize dust, and following specific cleanup and disposal procedures. Lead-based paint test kits are available at home improvement stores for around $10. For significant disturbance of lead paint — especially in homes with children or pregnant women — hire a certified lead abatement contractor.
</Warning>

<Tip>
  Keep a well-stocked first aid kit at your worksite at all times — not in the car, not in the other room, but right where you're working. At minimum it should include adhesive bandages in multiple sizes, gauze pads, medical tape, antiseptic wipes, antibiotic ointment, tweezers (for splinters), and an instant cold pack. For workshop environments, also include eye wash solution. Check and restock your kit every six months.
</Tip>

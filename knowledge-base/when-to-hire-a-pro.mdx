DIY saves money, builds skills, and gives you a real sense of ownership over your home — but it's only the right choice for the right jobs. Some home improvement tasks carry risks that go far beyond a bad-looking finish: electrical mistakes cause fires, improper structural changes cause collapse, and unpermitted work can make your home difficult to sell or uninsurable. Knowing where to draw the line isn't a sign of limitation — it's a sign of good judgment. This guide helps you make that call confidently, so you put your energy into projects that make sense for DIY and bring in professionals when the stakes require it.

## Always Hire a Professional

The following jobs should always be performed by licensed professionals, regardless of your skill level or how much you've watched on YouTube. These are non-negotiable.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Electrical Panel Work, New Circuits, and Rewiring — Licensed Electrician Required">
    Any work that involves your main electrical panel — adding circuits, replacing breakers, upgrading panel capacity, or touching service entrance wiring — must be performed by a licensed electrician. Similarly, running new circuits through walls, rewiring an older home, or adding outlets in locations that require new wiring should be handled by a pro. Electrical mistakes are among the leading causes of house fires, and improper wiring is invisible inside walls until it fails catastrophically. Permits and inspections are almost always required for this type of work.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Gas Lines and Gas Appliance Installation — Licensed Plumber or Gas Fitter Required">
    Any work involving natural gas or propane lines — running new lines, extending existing lines, connecting gas appliances, or servicing gas supply components — requires a licensed plumber or gas fitter with specific gas certifications. A gas leak is immediately life-threatening. Even experienced DIYers should not work on gas lines. This includes gas range installation (connecting the appliance to an existing properly sized shutoff is sometimes permitted as DIY — check local codes), gas dryer connections, and any buried or interior gas piping.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Structural Changes — Structural Engineer and Licensed Contractor Required">
    Removing walls (especially load-bearing walls), altering floor joists or roof rafters, modifying the foundation, adding structural openings for windows or doors, or building additions all require a structural engineer's assessment and a licensed general contractor in most jurisdictions. Load-bearing walls support the weight of your home above — removing one without proper beam support causes catastrophic structural failure. Never assume a wall is non-load-bearing; always have it assessed by a qualified professional.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="HVAC Installation and Refrigerant Work — Licensed HVAC Technician Required">
    Installing a new HVAC system, replacing a central air conditioner or heat pump, or performing any work that involves handling refrigerant requires a licensed HVAC technician. In the United States, Section 608 of the Clean Air Act requires EPA certification to purchase and handle refrigerants — it's not just a best practice, it's federal law. New system installation also involves complex load calculations, ductwork sizing, and electrical work that require professional expertise to get right.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Asbestos and Lead Paint Remediation — Certified Abatement Contractor Required">
    If testing confirms the presence of asbestos or lead paint in a material that must be disturbed, removed, or encapsulated, hire a certified abatement contractor. These contractors have the training, equipment, and legal authorization to handle these materials safely and dispose of them properly. This is not a cost-cutting opportunity — improper asbestos abatement can result in health damage to your family, contamination of your home, and significant legal liability. See the [Safety Guidelines](/knowledge-base/safety-guidelines) page for more on identifying these materials.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Roofing on Steep Pitches or Large Areas — Licensed Roofer Recommended">
    Working on a roof is inherently high-risk — falls from roofs are among the most serious and fatal DIY accidents. While small repairs on low-slope roofs may be manageable for experienced DIYers, full re-roofing, work on steep-pitch roofs (above 6:12), or any work on a two-story or taller home should be left to licensed roofing professionals with proper fall protection equipment, insurance, and experience. The cost of professional roofing is also a practical investment — a poor DIY roofing job can void your roofing material warranty and cause significant water damage.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Permitted Work — Ensure Licensed Contractor Pulls Permits">
    In most jurisdictions, permits for significant work must be pulled by the homeowner or a licensed contractor — and inspections must be completed before work is covered up. If your project requires a permit, ensure your contractor is properly licensed and will pull the permit in their name (or yours, where allowed). Never agree to let a contractor do permitted work "without pulling permits" to save money — the risk falls entirely on you as the homeowner.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Consider Hiring a Professional

These projects are technically possible as DIY tasks for skilled homeowners, but they come with complexity, risk, or equipment requirements that make hiring a professional worth serious consideration.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Complex Plumbing — New Drain Lines, Water Heater Replacement">
    Basic plumbing tasks (replacing fixtures, unclogging drains, replacing supply lines) are generally DIY-friendly. But cutting into existing drain-waste-vent (DWV) systems, extending supply lines to new locations, or installing a gas water heater crosses into territory where mistakes cause water damage, mold, and — in the case of gas — serious safety hazards. If you're replacing a tank water heater with a gas model, most municipalities require a licensed plumber and permit. Electric water heater replacement is more commonly done as DIY where codes allow.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Large or Complex Tile Jobs">
    Tiling a standard backsplash or small bathroom floor is manageable for an intermediate DIYer. But large-format tile (24"×24" or bigger), complex patterns (herringbone, Moroccan, custom layouts), heated floor system installation, or full wet-area shower tiling with proper waterproofing membranes demand a higher skill level. Waterproofing a shower incorrectly leads to water infiltration behind the walls — a slow-moving but very expensive problem. For complex or large tile work, a professional tile setter is worth the investment.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Hardwood Floor Refinishing">
    Refinishing hardwood floors is technically a DIY project — drum sanders and edge sanders are widely available for rent. But they are unforgiving: a moment of inattention with a drum sander can cut visible depressions in your floor that require professional correction. Staining and finish application also require a practiced hand for even results. If you have valuable hardwood floors, consider whether the cost savings justify the risk. If you're refinishing low-grade floors in a house you're about to sell, DIY makes more financial sense.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Full Kitchen or Bathroom Gut Renovation">
    A gut renovation — stripping a kitchen or bathroom down to studs and starting over — involves coordinating multiple trades (plumbing, electrical, tile, drywall, cabinetry, countertops) in the correct sequence, with permits, inspections, and tight timelines. Experienced DIYers can manage pieces of this work, but project managing an entire gut renovation while also doing the work is extremely demanding. Many homeowners find that acting as owner-contractor (hiring and coordinating individual subs while doing some work themselves) is a good middle path.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Exterior Painting — Two or More Stories">
    Painting the exterior of a single-story home is a manageable DIY project with the right ladder and extension tools. Two-story (and taller) homes introduce significant fall risk, require longer ladders or scaffolding, and involve large surface areas where quality control and consistent application become harder. Professional exterior painters complete these projects faster, with better equipment and insurance. If your exterior project involves heights above 12–14 feet, seriously consider getting at least one professional quote.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## How to Vet a Contractor

When you do hire a professional, doing your homework protects your money and your home.

**Check license and insurance.** Every contractor doing significant work on your home should carry a valid contractor's license in your state and general liability insurance (and workers' compensation if they have employees). Ask for the license number and verify it on your state's contractor licensing board website. Ask for a certificate of insurance — not just verbal confirmation.

**Get three quotes.** For any job over a few hundred dollars, get at least three written quotes from licensed contractors. This gives you a realistic sense of market price, surfaces quality differences between bidders, and gives you negotiating context. Be skeptical of quotes that are dramatically lower than the others — they usually reflect cut corners, missing scope, or a contractor who will find reasons to charge more later.

**Check reviews on multiple platforms.** Look up contractors on Google, Yelp, Angi (formerly Angie's List), Houzz, and the Better Business Bureau. Look for patterns in both positive and negative reviews — a few bad reviews among many good ones is normal; consistent complaints about communication, cost overruns, or incomplete work are red flags.

**Get everything in writing.** A signed contract should specify exactly what work will be done, what materials will be used (brand, model, grade), the project timeline with milestones, the total price, and the payment schedule. Verbal agreements are not enforceable. Change orders — any deviation from the original scope — should be signed and priced in writing before the work is done.

**Never pay more than 30% upfront.** A reasonable down payment (typically 10–30% depending on project size) to cover materials is standard and acceptable. Contractors who demand 50%, 75%, or full payment upfront before starting work are a significant red flag. Structure payments to milestones: a portion at start, a portion at rough-in or midpoint, and a final payment (ideally 10–15% of the total) held until the project is complete and you've had a chance to inspect the finished work.

<Warning>
  **Unpermitted work creates serious long-term problems for homeowners.** Work done without required permits cannot be legally inspected, may not meet current safety codes, and creates a documented gap in your home's improvement history. When you sell your home, unpermitted work must often be disclosed — and can be required to be brought up to code at your expense before closing. Unpermitted electrical, structural, or plumbing work can also void your homeowner's insurance policy for related claims. The cost of pulling a permit is almost always far less than the cost of dealing with unpermitted work later.
</Warning>

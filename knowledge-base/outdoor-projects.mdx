Your home's exterior is the first thing visitors, neighbors, and potential buyers see — and outdoor DIY projects often deliver some of the highest returns on your time and investment. From a freshly pressure-washed driveway to a stained deck or new garden border, the improvements you make outside are visible every single day. Outdoor projects also follow seasonal rhythms, so planning ahead means you're never scrambling to fix weatherstripping in January or sealing your deck in the rain. This guide covers the full range of outdoor DIY work, organized by category, with a seasonal maintenance checklist to keep your home's exterior in peak condition year-round.

## Exterior Maintenance Projects

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Painting or Staining the Exterior — Intermediate to Advanced">
    Exterior painting is one of the most impactful things you can do for curb appeal, but it's also one of the more demanding DIY projects. Proper prep — scraping peeling paint, washing the surface, priming bare wood, and caulking gaps — accounts for 70% of the work and 100% of the durability. Use a 100% acrylic exterior paint rated for your climate. For a single-story home, this is a manageable DIY project with a good extension ladder. Two-story homes are significantly more challenging and riskier — consider hiring a pro for the upper sections.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Pressure Washing Exterior Surfaces — Beginner">
    Pressure washing is one of the fastest and most satisfying DIY projects available. A single afternoon with a rented or purchased pressure washer can transform a driveway, walkway, siding, deck, or fence from grimy to clean. Use lower pressure settings (under 1,500 PSI) for wood and painted surfaces to avoid damage. Always work with the grain on wood and keep the nozzle moving. This is an excellent first step before painting, staining, or sealing any exterior surface.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Replacing Weatherstripping — Beginner">
    Worn or compressed weatherstripping around exterior doors and windows allows conditioned air to escape and outside air (and pests) to enter. Replacing it is inexpensive and takes less than an hour per door. Self-adhesive foam tape is the easiest option for door stops; V-strip (tension seal) weatherstripping is more durable for door sides. Door sweeps attach to the bottom of the door to seal the gap at the threshold. This is a great fall project before heating season begins.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Repairing Gutters and Downspouts — Beginner to Intermediate">
    Functional gutters are critical to protecting your foundation, siding, and landscaping from water damage. Common gutter repairs include resealing end caps and joints (use gutter sealant), straightening sagging sections by repositioning or adding gutter hangers, patching small holes with gutter repair tape or metal patch kits, and extending or redirecting downspouts away from the foundation. Clean gutters at least twice a year — spring and fall — to prevent clogs and overflow.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Caulking Exterior Windows and Doors — Beginner">
    Exterior caulk around windows, door frames, and where different siding materials meet prevents water infiltration and air leakage. Use a paintable exterior caulk (siliconized acrylic or polyurethane) rated for outdoor use. Cut the old caulk away, clean the surface, and apply a new bead, smoothing with a wet finger. This is a high-value fall project that reduces energy bills and prevents moisture damage. Budget 15–20 minutes per window and 20–30 minutes per door.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Deck and Fence Projects

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Staining or Sealing a Deck — Beginner to Intermediate">
    Staining or sealing your deck every two to three years is the most important maintenance task for preserving its life and appearance. A properly maintained deck can last decades; a neglected one becomes soft, gray, and dangerous within a few years. Start by cleaning the deck thoroughly (pressure washing or deck cleaner), allow it to dry for 48 hours, then apply your stain or sealer with a brush, roller, or pump sprayer. Semi-transparent stains offer the best combination of color, grain visibility, and protection.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Replacing Deck Boards — Intermediate">
    Individual deck boards can be replaced without rebuilding the entire deck. Identify boards that are soft, cracked, heavily checked, or pulling up fasteners. Use a pry bar and hammer to remove old boards, then cut and install new boards of matching dimensions. Composite decking is low-maintenance but requires specific fasteners; pressure-treated lumber should be allowed to dry before finishing. Pre-drill holes near board ends to prevent splitting.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Building or Repairing a Fence — Intermediate">
    A new fence or fence repair project is a satisfying weekend-to-weekend build that improves privacy, security, and curb appeal. For repairs, check that existing posts are plumb and firmly set — a loose post is the root cause of most fence failures. For new fence sections, set posts in concrete at least 2 feet deep (more in frost zones), allow to cure 24–48 hours, then attach rails and pickets. Check local codes for height limits and setback requirements before building.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Deck Cleaning and Prep — Beginner">
    A clean deck is the foundation of any staining, sealing, or painting project. Use a deck cleaner (oxygen bleach-based cleaners are effective and safer than chlorine bleach on wood) applied with a stiff brush or pump sprayer, then rinse thoroughly. For weathered gray wood, a deck brightener (oxalic acid solution) restores the natural wood tone. Allow the deck to dry fully — at least 48 hours in dry weather — before applying any finish.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Landscaping and Curb Appeal Projects

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Installing a Garden Bed Border — Beginner">
    Defined garden bed borders create clean lines that make your entire yard look more intentional and well-maintained. Options include steel edging (clean, modern, long-lasting), plastic landscape edging (budget-friendly), natural stone, or brick pavers. Use a flat spade or edging tool to cut a clean trench line before installing any type of border. This project pairs well with fresh mulch for maximum visual impact.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Laying Mulch — Beginner">
    A fresh layer of mulch in garden beds is one of the fastest curb appeal upgrades available. Mulch retains soil moisture, suppresses weeds, regulates soil temperature, and makes beds look clean and intentional. Apply 2–3 inches of mulch, keeping it pulled back a few inches from plant stems and tree trunks to prevent rot and pest issues. Shredded hardwood bark is the most popular option; cedar mulch adds natural pest-repellent properties.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Installing Landscape Lighting — Beginner to Intermediate">
    Low-voltage landscape lighting transforms your home's curb appeal at night and improves safety along walkways and steps. Solar-powered path lights require no wiring and can be installed in minutes. Hardwired low-voltage systems (12V transformer with buried cable) require more planning but offer more reliable, consistent lighting. Most transformer systems are plug-in and straightforward to set up — run the cable, stake the lights, connect, and set the timer.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Planting Trees and Shrubs — Beginner">
    Strategic tree and shrub planting is a long-term investment in your home's value, energy efficiency, and beauty. Shade trees on the west and south sides of your home can meaningfully reduce summer cooling costs. When planting, dig a hole two to three times wider than the root ball but no deeper — planting too deep is the most common mistake. Water thoroughly after planting and apply a 2–3 inch mulch ring (keeping it away from the trunk) to retain moisture during establishment.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Seasonal Home Exterior Maintenance Checklist

Staying ahead of seasonal maintenance prevents small problems from becoming expensive repairs. Use this checklist as your annual guide.

### 🌱 Spring
- [ ] Inspect the roof for winter damage — missing shingles, lifted flashing, damaged vents
- [ ] Clean and inspect gutters and downspouts — flush with water to check flow
- [ ] Inspect exterior caulk around windows, doors, and penetrations — recaulk where cracked or missing
- [ ] Check the deck for soft spots, loose fasteners, and winter damage — plan staining if needed
- [ ] Walk the foundation perimeter — look for cracks, water staining, or settled soil near the house
- [ ] Inspect exterior paint or siding for peeling, cracking, or damage from winter

### ☀️ Summer
- [ ] Check and clean the irrigation system — inspect heads, adjust coverage, look for leaks
- [ ] Touch up exterior paint on trim, doors, and any areas showing wear
- [ ] Schedule or perform AC maintenance — clean condenser coils, change filter, check refrigerant lines for insulation damage
- [ ] Treat the deck or fence if the finish is showing wear
- [ ] Check window screens — repair or replace damaged screens

### 🍂 Fall
- [ ] Replace or upgrade weatherstripping on all exterior doors
- [ ] Clean gutters after leaves fall — install gutter guards if cleaning is a recurring burden
- [ ] Disconnect and drain garden hoses; shut off exterior hose bibs (frost-free models should still be drained)
- [ ] Caulk any gaps around windows and doors before heating season
- [ ] Test smoke and CO detectors; replace batteries
- [ ] Inspect and clean the dryer vent

### ❄️ Winter
- [ ] Insulate exposed pipes in unheated spaces (crawlspace, garage, exterior walls)
- [ ] Check and service the heating system — replace filter, schedule furnace inspection if not done in fall
- [ ] Seal visible drafts around outlets, switches, and penetrations on exterior walls using foam gaskets
- [ ] Keep gutters clear of ice dams in snow-prone climates — consider heated cable for chronic problem areas
- [ ] Check attic insulation and ventilation — inadequate insulation is the main cause of ice dams

<Warning>
  **Permits are often required for decks, fences, and outbuildings.** Most municipalities require a building permit for new decks (and sometimes for significant deck repairs or additions), fences over a certain height, sheds over a certain square footage, and any work that involves structural changes. Permit requirements vary significantly by location — always check with your local building department before starting any of these projects. Building without a required permit can result in fines, mandatory demolition, and complications when selling your home.
</Warning>

<Tip>
  Before and after every major exterior project, take photographs — ideally from the same vantage points. These photos serve multiple purposes: they document the work you completed (useful if questions arise during a home sale), provide evidence for insurance claims if exterior damage occurs, and give you a satisfying record of your progress over time. Store photos in a dedicated home maintenance folder in your cloud storage or the DIYHomie app.
</Tip>

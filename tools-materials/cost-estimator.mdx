One of the most common reasons DIY projects stall is running into unexpected costs midway through. The DIYHomie Cost Estimator helps you avoid that by giving you a realistic, itemized budget before you buy a single thing. It pulls your project's full materials list, factors in tool costs, accounts for regional pricing differences, and even shows you how much you'd save compared to hiring a contractor. Going in with clear numbers makes it easier to plan, prioritize, and finish what you start.

## How the Cost Estimator Works

When you run an estimate, DIYHomie combines three data sources to build your budget:

1. **Your project's materials list** — quantities and specifications pulled directly from the project steps
2. **Regional pricing data** — average retail prices adjusted for your zip code, so your estimate reflects what things actually cost near you
3. **Labor context** — for hybrid projects where you might hire out one phase (like electrical rough-in), the estimator surfaces typical local labor rates for that portion only

The result is a line-by-line cost breakdown you can review, adjust, and export.

## Using the Cost Estimator

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open a project">
    Go to the project you want to estimate from your **Projects** tab or search the project library. The Cost Estimator is available on all projects, including ones you've customized.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Tap Estimate Cost">
    On the project overview screen, tap **Estimate Cost**. On web, find it in the **Project Tools** panel on the right side of the screen.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Enter your zip code">
    Type in your zip code when prompted. This enables regional pricing adjustments and ensures your estimate reflects local retail averages rather than a national baseline.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review the itemized breakdown">
    Browse your full cost breakdown, organized into three sections: **Materials**, **Tools**, and **Optional Labor**. Each line item shows the unit cost, quantity, and line total. The summary at the top shows your projected total DIY cost.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Adjust quantities if needed">
    Tap any line item to edit the quantity — for example, if you already own some of a material or plan to buy a larger pack size. The total updates automatically as you make changes.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Cost Breakdown Sections

### Materials
Every material required by the project, including fasteners, adhesives, finishing supplies, and consumables. Quantities are pre-populated from the project scope and adjusted for standard waste or overage where applicable.

### Tools (Buy vs. Rent)
Tools are listed separately from materials, and each one includes both a **purchase price estimate** and a **rental price estimate** (where rental is commonly available). This lets you make an informed buy-vs.-rent decision for each tool based on your situation.

### Labor (If Hiring a Pro for Part)
For tasks that homeowners frequently hire out — such as electrical panel work, gas line connections, or structural changes — the estimator includes an optional labor line with a typical local rate range. This section is informational; it helps you decide which phases to DIY and which to delegate.

## DIY Savings Comparison

At the bottom of your estimate, DIYHomie shows a **DIY Savings** figure: the difference between what a contractor would typically charge for the full project and your projected DIY cost. This comparison uses regional labor and markup data and is meant to give you a sense of scale — not a guaranteed quote.

<Note>
  All estimates are based on average retail prices and regional data updated periodically. They may not reflect current sales, closeout pricing, store-brand alternatives, or significant local price variation. Use your estimate as a planning baseline and confirm final costs before purchasing.
</Note>

<Warning>
  Some projects require permits, inspections, or both — and those fees are not included in the cost estimate. Permit costs vary widely by municipality and project type. Check your local building department's requirements before starting any structural, electrical, plumbing, or HVAC work.
</Warning>

<Tip>
  Want a quick ballpark before opening a project? Ask the AI assistant: **"How much does it cost to replace a bathroom faucet myself?"** It will give you a cost range, a rough materials list, and flag any tools you might need to factor in.
</Tip>
